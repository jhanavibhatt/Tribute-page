Swami Vivekananda

"Arise, awake, and stop not till the goal is reached."

Introduction

Swami Vivekananda (1863-1902) was a Hindu monk and spiritual leader who is best known for his role in introducing Hindu philosophy and Vedanta to the Western world. He was a disciple of the Indian mystic Ramakrishna and founded the Ramakrishna Math and Mission in 1897. Vivekananda was a prolific writer and speaker and is considered one of the most influential figures of the Indian Renaissance.

Early Life

Vivekananda was born into an aristocratic family in Kolkata (formerly Calcutta), India, in 1863. His given name was Narendranath Datta. He was educated at the Presidency College in Kolkata and later at the Scottish Church College. As a young man, he was interested in the Western rationalist tradition and was also drawn to the teachings of Ramakrishna, a mystic who lived in Kolkata.

Teachings

Vivekananda's teachings are based on the Vedanta philosophy of Hinduism, which emphasizes the unity of all beings and the ultimate reality of the universe. He believed that the highest goal of human life is to realize one's true nature as a divine being and to attain liberation from the cycle of birth and death. Vivekananda's message was one of self-empowerment and social reform, and he emphasized the importance of education and spiritual practice in achieving these goals.

Legacy

Vivekananda's influence has been felt in many different fields, including spirituality, philosophy, education, and social reform. His writings and speeches continue to inspire people all over the world, and the Ramakrishna Math and Mission he founded has become a major force for social and educational reform in India.
